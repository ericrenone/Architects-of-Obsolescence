# Architects of Obsolescence
## The Moral Reckoning Adults Owe Children in the Age of Machine Intelligence

> *"The planner over-invests in skills destined for obsolescence, a distortion that increases monotonically with AI prevalence."*
> — Peterson, 2025 (arXiv:2508.19625)

> *"Every child who wastes a year mastering what a machine already does perfectly is not failing. The adults who designed that child's incentive structure are."*

---

## The Central Claim

This is not a guide about artificial intelligence.

It is a moral document about adult responsibility.

The research is settled. The benchmark record is public. The labor market data is peer-reviewed. Every adult with the capacity to influence a child's educational trajectory has access to the same evidence. The question is no longer what is happening — it is who is accountable for allowing it to continue.

This README makes the case that adults — parents, educators, administrators, policymakers, and prestige-granting institutions — bear direct moral culpability for directing children's cognitive capital toward domains that machines permanently mastered years ago. The failure is not ignorance. It is willful inattention to available evidence, motivated by cultural comfort, status path dependency, and the confusion of visible effort with productive effort.

That confusion has a cost. The cost is borne entirely by children.

## 1. The Knowability Condition: Why Ignorance Is Not an Excuse

Moral culpability typically requires two conditions: the capacity to know, and the failure to act on that knowledge. Both are satisfied here.

### What Adults Can Know

The machine capability record is not classified, technical, or contested. It is maintained in public leaderboards updated in real time by the global AI evaluation community. The saturation record as of 2026:

| Benchmark | Domain | Frontier Score |
|---|---|---|
| GSM8K | Grade-school arithmetic | ~99% |
| MATH-500 | Competition mathematics | ~96% |
| HumanEval | Code generation | ~93% |
| SWE-bench Verified | Real software engineering | ~100% |
| AIME 2025 / 2026 | Mathematics Olympiad | 100% (GPT-5) |
| GPQA Diamond | Graduate science reasoning | ~94% |

Spelling calculation and chess game-tree search do not appear on this table. They are absent not because they are difficult, but because they were beneath the evaluation community's measurement floor before transformer architectures existed. They were solved earlier, more completely, and more cheaply. A $0.002 API call now outperforms a decade of human drilling.

This information is public. Any adult who claims not to know it has chosen not to look.

### The Moral Weight of Available Evidence

Moral philosophy distinguishes between factual ignorance (genuinely unavailable information) and willful ignorance (available information not consulted). Thomas Aquinas's treatment of vincible ignorance is directly relevant: ignorance that results from insufficient diligence, when diligence was both possible and required, does not excuse the agent. It compounds the fault.

A parent who spends $50,000 on spelling bee coaches without spending two hours reading the benchmark saturation record is not innocent. They are in vincible ignorance: the information was available, the stakes demanded inquiry, and the inquiry was not made.

## 2. The Virtue Ethics Case: Phronesis and the Duty of Practical Wisdom

The Aristotelian virtue most directly implicated here is *phronesis* — practical wisdom, the capacity to discern the right action in particular circumstances, especially when circumstances are complex and changing. Phronesis is not a fixed rule system; it is the cultivated capacity to see what genuinely matters in a specific situation and act accordingly.

### The Parent's Phronesis Obligation

Aristotle treats the rearing of children as the paradigm case of phronesis in action. The *Politics* grounds parental authority in the child's incapacity for full rational agency — the parent exercises judgment on behalf of someone who cannot yet exercise it themselves. This trusteeship relationship creates a correspondingly elevated duty: the parent who exercises poor judgment in directing a child's development is not merely making a personal mistake. They are abusing a delegated authority.

Phronesis requires what Aristotle calls *perception of particulars* — seeing the relevant features of the specific situation rather than applying general rules blindly. The relevant particular here is the benchmark saturation record. A parent of practical wisdom, confronted with evidence that a machine executes a skill at zero marginal cost with perfect accuracy, would ask: *what is the point of training my child to do this?* The phronimos does not need a philosopher to answer that question. The answer is visible to anyone who looks.

The parent who does not look — who accepts the prestige signal of the spelling bee trophy without examining whether the skill it rewards generates human value — is failing the Aristotelian test. They are substituting conventional approval for practical wisdom. This is the failure Aristotle associates with *doxophilia*: the love of reputation over the love of the good.

### The Educator's Phronesis Obligation

Teachers and school administrators occupy a position of professional phronesis: they are trusted to make pedagogical judgments on behalf of students and the broader community. Professional practical wisdom carries a higher bar than lay judgment. A physician who prescribes a treatment known to be obsolete is not merely wrong — they have violated a professional duty.

The educator who rewards spelling bee achievement as intellectual achievement, in 2026, knowing what the benchmark record shows, is in an analogous position. They are validating a proxy for intellectual seriousness that has been severed from its referent. The credential signals effort; it no longer signals capability at anything a machine cannot do for free.

### Virtue and the Formation of Character

There is a deeper virtue ethics dimension that transcends the instrumental argument. Aristotelian ethics holds that the virtues are developed through practice in appropriate activities. The child who spends thousands of hours in bounded, rule-following, automatically-gradable performance develops cognitive habits calibrated to that environment.

The habits that closed-world training cultivates:

- Rapid retrieval of bounded rules
- Confidence in deterministic, verifiable domains
- Aversion to ambiguity and underspecification
- Performance optimization under fixed evaluation criteria

These are precisely the habits that transfer most poorly to the open-world, multi-constraint, ambiguity-laden environments where human cognitive premium concentrates in an AI economy. The child does not merely learn a useless skill. They are habituated into a cognitive character structure that is misaligned with the demands of the world they will inherit.

Character formation, once complete, is not easily reversed. The adult who installs the wrong cognitive habits in a child has done something that compounds over time. This is the most serious dimension of the moral failure: it is not merely a misallocation of training hours. It is the formation of a person whose cognitive dispositions are structurally mismatched to their environment.

## 3. The Deontological Case: Parental Duty and the Corruption of Care

Kantian ethics grounds parental obligation in the child's status as a rational being in development. Parents do not own children. They hold them in trust. The categorical imperative, in its second formulation — treat rational beings always as ends, never merely as means — applies with particular force to the parent-child relationship.

### The Corruption of Genuine Care

Genuine parental care is directed at the child's actual flourishing — their development as a capable, autonomous rational agent who can navigate their world effectively. Corrupted care substitutes the parent's desire for social validation for the child's genuine interest. The corruption is often unconscious, which does not diminish its moral weight.

When a parent directs a child toward a spelling bee because the competition produces visible, legible, socially rewarded achievement, while declining to examine whether that achievement reflects genuine developmental value, the parent is — at least in part — using the child as an instrument of their own social status. The trophy goes in the parents' living room. The misallocated thousands of hours are borne entirely by the child.

Kant's test: could the parent universalize the maxim "I will direct my child toward activities that maximize visible achievement signals, without examining whether those signals track genuine human capability in the current technological environment"? The universalized world is one in which an entire generation is trained for obsolescence while the machines that have made their training obsolete become more capable every quarter. That world is not one a rational agent would will.

### The Non-Interference Duty

Deontological ethics also implies a negative duty: do not impede the development of a child's rational agency. Installing cognitive habits that are structurally misaligned with the child's environment is not a neutral act. It is an active interference with their capacity for autonomous navigation of the world they will inhabit. The hours spent drilling Sanskrit roots are hours not spent developing epistemic auditing, structural problem framing, or adversarial reasoning — the capacities that will determine whether the child can function independently in an AI-intensive economy.

This opportunity cost is not merely economic. It is developmental. Cognitive habits formed in childhood resist revision. The interference compounds.

## 4. The Consequentialist Case: Quantified Harm, Identifiable Agents

Utilitarian ethics requires that we consider the actual consequences of actions, weighted by probability and magnitude. The consequences here are neither speculative nor small.

### The Individual Child

Peterson's (2025) formal model predicts a labor market skill mismatch that increases monotonically with AI prevalence. The child whose training concentrates in saturated capability domains enters an economy where those skills carry no wage premium above the automated baseline. The precise magnitude is contested; the direction is not. The expected return on spelling bee preparation, measured in differentiated human labor market value, is approaching zero.

The investment is not zero. The median competitive spelling bee participant spends 1,000–3,000 hours in preparation. At the opportunity cost of equivalent time invested in agentic system architecture, epistemic auditing, or structural problem framing — skills that the labor market is actively repricing upward — the loss is concrete and large.

### The Aggregate

Restuccia & Rogerson's (2017) analysis of factor misallocation establishes the macroeconomic stakes. Routing high-productivity inputs to low-productivity uses is a primary driver of total factor productivity gaps across economies — not a marginal inefficiency but a compounding structural drag. When a nation's most motivated, disciplined, high-potential adolescents concentrate their cognitive investment in domains that AI executes at zero cost, the aggregate productivity loss is not confined to those individuals. The foregone R&D capacity, institutional design capability, and systems-level thinking that those hours could have produced represents a macro-level drag that accumulates across cohorts.

The prestige system that produces this misallocation is an institutional friction in Restuccia & Rogerson's technical sense. The agents who maintain and reward it bear responsibility for the friction's consequences.

### Identifiable Agents, Not Diffuse Systems

A common evasion in cases of structural harm is the diffusion of responsibility across "the system" to the point where no individual agent is held accountable. This evasion is not available here.

The following agents make specific, identifiable decisions that maintain the misallocation:

- **Parents** who fund spelling bee preparation and chess coaching without examining the opportunity cost
- **Educators** who weight spelling bee and chess achievement in academic recognition and admissions recommendations
- **Admissions officers** who treat these achievements as positive intellectual signals
- **Corporate sponsors** who provide prize money and media coverage for these competitions
- **Institutional administrators** who allocate extracurricular resources and prestige toward these activities
- **Media organizations** that frame drilling Sanskrit roots as a human interest story about discipline without noting its opportunity cost

Each of these agents makes a discrete, revisable decision. None of them is compelled by the structure of the world to make the decision they are making. Each could look at the benchmark saturation record and ask what they are rewarding and why. That they do not is a choice.

## 5. The Four Structural Moral Failures

### Failure I — The Effort-Outcome Confusion

Adults systematically reward visible effort rather than productive effort. A child who drills 40,000 words demonstrates effort that is highly visible: the hours are long, the feedback is immediate, the performance is public and emotionally charged. A child who develops epistemic auditing capacity demonstrates effort that is largely invisible: it involves reading research papers, testing AI outputs against domain knowledge, maintaining calibration records, and building mental models of failure modes.

The first child gets a trophy. The second gets nothing a parent can put on a shelf.

This is not a minor cultural quirk. It is a systematic misalignment between reward and value that is maintained, renewed, and amplified by adult decisions. Adults who reward visible effort over productive effort are teaching children that the former is what matters. They are wrong, and the children pay for the error.

### Failure II — The Prestige Inertia Problem

Prestige attaches to pathways that have historically filtered for demonstrated ability. Spelling bees and chess tournaments have filtered for talented, disciplined children for generations. The filtering function was real. But the filtering function is now severed from its referent: the skills being filtered for no longer map to human cognitive comparative advantage.

Adults who maintain prestige signals past the point where they track genuine value are not merely making an error. They are actively deceiving the children whose behavior they are shaping. A prestige signal that once meant "this child is developing capabilities that will generate value" and now means "this child is optimizing for a domain that machines dominate" is a lie. Adults who issue it, repeat it, and reward it bear responsibility for that deception.

### Failure III — The Status Laundering Problem

A specific and pervasive form of the moral failure is the use of children's achievement as a vehicle for adult status. When a parent publicizes a spelling bee championship on social media, the primary beneficiary of the attention is the parent. The child provides the performance; the parent captures the reputational return.

This is not categorically wrong. Parents appropriately take pride in their children's achievements. It becomes morally problematic when the adult's reputational interest in a visible, legible achievement overrides the genuine inquiry into whether that achievement serves the child's development. The parent who would be embarrassed to tell their social circle that their child spent three years building epistemic auditing skills — because the skill is invisible, unjudgeable, and trophy-less — is prioritizing their own social comfort over their child's genuine formation. That is a moral failure with a name: it is vanity, in the specific sense that Aristotelian ethics gives to the vice of prioritizing appearance over reality.

### Failure IV — The Epistemic Negligence Problem

Adults who make significant resource allocation decisions on behalf of children have a duty of epistemic diligence proportionate to the stakes. The stakes here are high: thousands of hours of a child's cognitive development during the most plastic years of their life.

An adult who allocates $30,000 in tutoring fees and 2,000 hours of a child's time to a competitive activity without consulting the available research on whether that activity develops human-differentiated capability is not exercising due diligence. The research exists. The benchmark saturation record is public. The labor market projections are published. The adult who does not consult them is engaging in what philosophers of epistemology call epistemic negligence: the culpable failure to gather available evidence before making decisions with significant consequences for others.

Epistemic negligence does not excuse the agent. It compounds the fault. The agent who made an uninformed decision could have been informed. They chose not to be.

## 6. The Prestige System as Moral Apparatus

The deepest moral analysis targets not individual parents or educators but the institutional prestige system that structures their incentives. Prestige systems are moral apparatuses: they encode judgments about what is valuable, what is admirable, and what is worthy of investment. When a prestige system encodes false judgments, it produces systematic moral harm at scale.

The current prestige system encodes a false judgment: that performance in bounded, rule-complete, automatically-gradable cognitive tasks is an indicator of intellectual seriousness. This judgment was approximately true for most of human history, because such tasks were the only ones that could be rigorously measured. It became false when machines mastered those tasks completely.

The prestige system has not updated. The benchmark saturation record has.

### Who Maintains the System

Prestige systems are not natural facts. They are maintained by specific agents making specific decisions:

- The *New York Times* that publishes the spelling bee as an admirable human interest story
- The corporation that provides $50,000 in prize money
- The university that treats the championship as a positive admissions signal
- The school that nominates the spelling champion for the academic achievement award
- The parent who frames the trophy as evidence of intellectual excellence

Each of these agents is a node in the prestige network. Each makes a revisable decision. Collectively they constitute the maintenance apparatus of a system that is directing high-potential human cognitive capital toward zero-value deployment.

The maintenance of a false prestige system is a moral act. It has victims. Those victims are identifiable. The agents who maintain the system are responsible for the consequences.

## 7. What Morally Adequate Adult Behavior Looks Like

Moral obligation is not discharged by diagnosis alone. The following constitutes the minimum threshold of morally adequate adult behavior in the current environment.

### For Parents

**Epistemic diligence.** Before allocating significant resources to a child's cognitive training — money, time, opportunity cost — consult the available evidence on whether that domain develops human-differentiated capability in the current technological environment. The benchmark saturation record is publicly available. The labor market task literature is peer-reviewed and accessible. Ignorance of available evidence is not a defense. Consult it.

**The phronesis question.** For any competitive or intensive training activity, ask: *could a frontier AI system execute this task at performance levels exceeding 90% of trained human competitors?* If yes, examine whether the genuine developmental value — persistence, discipline, competitive resilience — could be routed through a vehicle that points at the right cognitive targets. The answer is almost always yes. The routing is the parent's responsibility.

**Separate effort from outcome.** Praise and reward the character traits that intensive training develops — persistence, discipline, tolerance for struggle — while being honest about whether the specific vehicle is developing capability that will matter. The child can be told: *you are building real qualities through this work. We are going to make sure those qualities are pointed in the right direction.*

**The status audit.** Examine honestly whether a child's activity is being maintained partly because it generates social recognition for the parent. If it is, that is not automatically disqualifying — but it is a conflict of interest that must be consciously managed. The child's developmental interest, not the parent's reputational interest, is the decision criterion.

### For Educators

**The diagnostic question.** Apply annually to every major curriculum component: *can a frontier AI system execute this learning objective at performance levels exceeding 90% of students completing the current curriculum?* If yes, the component is a candidate for redesign — not elimination, but redesign toward a vehicle that develops the same character traits while targeting human-differentiated cognitive capabilities.

**Reform assessment.** Current assessment structures reward content recall and bounded problem-solving — the exact skill profile of automation. The minimum reform is introducing assessment components that are structurally impossible to outsource to AI: open-world problem framing, epistemic audit portfolios, multi-stakeholder communication records, adversarial reasoning documentation. These reveal genuine cognitive capability in ways that vocabulary drills and chess ratings do not.

**Honest prestige signals.** An educator who nominates a spelling champion for an academic achievement award is issuing a prestige signal. That signal shapes the choices of other children and other parents. Issuing a prestige signal that encodes a false judgment about intellectual value is a professional failure. The professional duty is to ensure that institutional recognition tracks genuine capability development.

### For Policymakers and Institutions

**Reform extracurricular weighting in admissions.** University admissions that treat spelling bee and chess achievements as intellectual markers are rewarding the wrong cognitive profile. The reform is not to ignore discipline and competitive achievement — it is to recognize them through vehicles that demonstrate human-differentiated capability: open-world problem portfolios, epistemic audit records, agentic problem-solving competitions. These cannot be won by AI proxies, which is itself the evidence that they track the right capability.

**Fund the right competitions.** National spelling bees and scholastic chess tournaments receive federal recognition and corporate sponsorship. The same resources — prize structures, media coverage, institutional prestige — should be applied to competitions that reward epistemic auditing, structural problem framing, and agentic system architecture. The competitive apparatus is pedagogically powerful. It is pointed at the wrong target.

**Connect curriculum approval to forward labor market signals.** Peterson's (2025) core policy recommendation is that AI adoption in education must be paired with explicit forward-looking labor market evidence. Curriculum approval processes should require documentation of how learning objectives map to projected skill demand, with explicit exclusion of objectives falling within saturated AI capability domains.

## 9. The Obligations That Follow

The moral argument in this document generates concrete obligations at every level of adult authority over children's development.

**The obligation of inquiry.** Every adult who makes significant decisions about a child's cognitive training has an obligation to consult the available evidence about whether that training develops human-differentiated capability. This is not optional. It is the minimum threshold of epistemic diligence proportionate to the stakes.

**The obligation of honesty.** Every adult who issues prestige signals — through prize money, institutional recognition, admissions weighting, media coverage, or social approval — has an obligation to ensure those signals encode accurate judgments about genuine capability development. Issuing false prestige signals at scale is a moral act with identifiable victims.

**The obligation of reallocation.** Every adult who has been directing a child toward a saturated cognitive domain has an obligation to examine whether the genuine developmental value of that activity — the persistence, discipline, and competitive drive it builds — can be preserved while redirecting the vehicle toward human-differentiated cognitive targets. This redesign is almost always possible. The obligation to pursue it is clear.

**The obligation of systemic action.** The individual adult who reforms their personal decisions while leaving the prestige system intact has done something good but not enough. The prestige system is maintained by collective decisions. Changing it requires collective action: speaking in admissions committees, publishing in educational journals, redirecting corporate sponsorship, reforming curriculum approval processes, and redesigning the competitions we run and celebrate.

The children are not failing.

The adults who designed their incentive structures are. The evidence is available. The obligations are clear. The reallocation is overdue.
